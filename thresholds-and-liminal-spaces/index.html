Thresholds and Liminal Spaces

2 min read

Our dojo is a temporary space:– during the day it’s a dusty village hall; in the evenings, it becomes a sacred space of learning.

Before the dojo exists, there is only Outside (a community hall, profane existence, the usual). We create the Inside, the dojo, by passing over the threshold - through liminal space. Literally, of course, each student passes through the doorway, and many schools of martial arts require that students pause to bow in the doorway, but this liminal space is larger and longer (temporally) than the doorway itself.

Our liminal space is created by a process, a series of steps that serve to bring the participants from the outside, the profane world, to the inside, the sacred moment. The steps exist as rings around our central stage, each of which demands attention and negotiation before one can move on to the next:

  • Entering the room
  • Cleaning the room
  • Laying down the mats
  • Putting up the signs
  • Changing our clothes
  • Bowing before stepping on to the mat
  • Lining up in seiza (kneeling), bowing
  • Warming up together

Of course, any one of these exists as a mundane step that just has to be done - you can’t practise in your work clothes comfortably, so you change into something appropriate for the occasion. At the same time, it also exists as a doorway through which one can catch a glimpse of another world. Of course, the Aikido kit (kidogi) is a sturdy cotton material appropriate for physical activity, pulling and pushing, AND it transports us to a world of martial spirit, crisp lines, cleanliness, and death. Kidogi is traditional Japanese underwear, so it’s not surprising that it’s white, much like my string vest. However, white is also the traditional Japanese colour of death, so we are also garbing ourselves in funereal attire to prepare for death.

In Robert Bly’s exploration of men and masculinity, Iron John, the hero of the tale sets off to war on a three-legged nag, but the wild man of the woods (Iron John himself) provides him with a four-legged, nostril-flaring and -steaming, ground-thumping steed, shining and powerful. This speaks to the transformation that we experience (usually in retrospect) during our practice - we come in, limping from a day in the office, perhaps tired, perhaps a bit overwhelmed. By the end of the class, we’ve often experienced a moment of our own brilliance (or witnessed our partner’s), and return to the outside world refreshed, re-girded, and full spirited.

By returning to this ritual space week after week, year after year, we start to bring our transformed brilliance out with us when we return to the outside world. We start to walk taller, we begin to honour and speak our truths, we withstand discomfort without collapsing or pushing it away. We begin to live from our Inside - the Inside of the dojo has created a space for and awareness of our inner warrior. We find that we have a guide within us who seeks to help us navigate through the Outside.

And the death? Every time we fall over, every time we fail, we have an opportunity to die a little on the mat. And every single small death is a shedding of an old skin, bringing a new, lithe skin to the surface. Every small death invites us to let go of the old knowledge and experience, and to open up to new possibilities. This, then, is a death to be sought out and celebrated!

— Falling uphill —
