4 January 2019 / Walking

The land welcomes you

3 min read

Welcome to my home ...

We have a couple of self-catering chalets on our 2-acre plot, which are usually fully booked for the spring and summer. That means we meet two new sets of strangers each week.

They come, often tired at the end of a long journey, to pick up the key. Or if we're out, we pop by to say hello when we return home. Either way, there is a moment of meeting for the first time.

I smile, introduce myself, and offer my hand.

The responses to that are varied.

Not everyone has taken my hand, though most do. Many take it and they look away; others shake it over an imaginary fence. Some barely touch it; others wring the life out of it. Yet others twist their wrist so that their hand holds mine down.

Funny, I think – you've come here on holiday, and brought all your shit with you.

For them, I think, the relationship is transactional and necessary ("where should we collect the keys from?"). For us, it isn't, and that's what I want to explore.

When we moved here, looking at the huge oak and chestnut trees, wandering down to the river that has scoured seams of slate for millenia, studying the moss growing over a tumble-down rock wall, even looking at the rusted ironwork along the railway, I soon realised that we did not own this. If we insisted on putting ourselves at the centre, then we had become the stewards of this space, but that was still some human-centric stretch from the real feeling of the place.

We had become part of a landscape (though even that word is misleading), just as the moss growing on the wall, just as the bugs crawling under the moss and the buzzard hovering above it. We belonged to it, not the other way round.

In the English language at least, we put humans and human experience first. Subject - verb - object, every time. I live here. Primacy for the human, all else relegated to background.

But that's not my experience.

Frank Forencich of Exuberant Animal writes of the Long Body as human body intimately connected to habitat and tribe. Well, here we have both tribe – in our nuclear family and our paying guests – and we have habitat, the larger context within which we exist.

And I don't think our guests know that. They come, they holiday, they go. We stay, we welcome new strangers, we feel the differences between the holiday-makers from week to week, both face-to-face and through the feeling of the space of our property.

From Spring to mid-Autumn, there are short-stay strangers here most of the time. We may or may not see them daily, but they're present either physically or in our awareness. It is noticeable when the last guests of the year leave, and the human-ness of this space shrinks and quietens so that we become more aware of the natural nature of this space. The birds, the trees, the weather are less backgrounded once the people have gone.

For myself, my eyes soften – I can see without looking – and my ears relax, allowing me to hear without needing to listen. I can receive just by being, not needing to seek anything out.

(Which is not to say that's what I do. Clearly, I spend a huge amount of time spinning thoughts around in my head to little effect).

In other words, it becomes clearer that my long body includes strangers only after they've gone and we're in the shorter days of the year. Then the local world becomes a bit quieter and proportionately less human. The light of day is somewhat dimmed so that the sharp outlines and contrasts of spring and summer aren't so crisp. Noises are muffled by the quietness of birds and the increased hiss of the river's flow as more rain falls. Even midday walking-the-dog takes on a gloom.

— Falling uphill —
