Think of your One Point

2 min read

This is frequently the very first instruction I give to a new student. They tense their abdomen, and even prod around it, until they become aware of a place where they can't find resistance. That absence of tension defines the One Point (the seika tanden, also the seika no itten).

But my view of it has changed enough over the years for me to think that I misunderstood the instruction when I first heard it. I was prodding down the front of my abdomen, seeking a lack of tension at or just under my skin, which I didn't quite find so I felt the One Point to be a vague, nebulous concept.

In the intervening years, as I taken up sports and activities that require more core strength, I've learnt about the layers of muscles and tissues that create lines of tension/strength/power in different directions around the abdomen. And, of course, deep inside those layers there are cavities full of organs. By definition, these areas cannot tense up; they can only be squeezed by the muscles around them.

So, now I look for my One Point in the middle of a hollow core of strength created by multiple lines of muscles and tissues pulling in opposing and complementary directions.

My best analogy so far is two-fold. Perhaps, as a child, you had pencil, paper, and ruler. Perhaps you drew a straight line, and then another just overlapping it but in a slightly different direction. And another, and another, until all these straight lines combined to sketch the outline of a rough circle.

The circle exists only as the result of multiple lines pulling in different directions.

Now, let's give the analogy a third dimension â€“ consider a leather football. It is made up of multiple small hexagonal panels and stitching. But the ball has a definite centre. There's nothing other than air inside the ball, and yet its centre is real. Geometrically, the ball has a centre.

That centre exists and doesn't, at the same time.

Slice the ball in two, and there's nothing to be found. Put them back together, press on one side of the ball, and the opposite side will respond equally â€“ the pressure has travelled through the centre.

So could help me make sense of the One Point? For a long time, the Chinese have referred to the dantien, and the Japanese to the tanden, as the sphere of power (strength, energy) in the lower abdomen. Of course, there is strength there expressed through the contraction of the multiple muscles in the area. There is also a central point of emptiness in the middle, that performs as the central point around which the tanden rotates.

When you Think of your One Point, you make that point the origin for subsequent movement, giving both power and balance to it, strength and stability.

Perhaps the tanden is the compass; the One Point is the central point on which the two-headed compass arrow rests and rotates. Perhaps the tanden is the spinning top; the One Point is the legendary point of stillness at the centre of the spinning.
