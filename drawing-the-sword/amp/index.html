Falling uphill

Drawing the sword

by James Knight

2016-04-14

Well, truth be told, I had drunk too much wine the night before, so was  decidedly fragile when your eyes landed on me to say it was my turn in  the spotlight. Never mind: it added another layer of complexity to my  study.

It was really, really hard to start in that room, with that group of  people sitting variously around the floor. Drawing the sword is a deliberately intense practice, and I hadn’t realised just how much  intensity comes from the presence of others equally engaged in the  process. On this occasion, all the intensity had to come from me, rather  than me joining and amplifying what is already in the space.

I knelt. I paused. I bowed.

You asked what does it mean?

My immediate response was a blank. I don’t know what it means because I  have never asked. I simply do what my teacher tells me. That obedience  leads me to know what it means at a deep level in my body, but that I  don’t have the words for it waiting in my head. A momentary panic, then  settling, and then words start to emerge: Death, Spirit.

Death

It means death, so you enquire: can it  be a balance of life and death?

Well, yes and no. It is not a balance of the two, because there is no  separation of the two: the two are the same thing, but they look  different. By engaging intensely with death, we live. My desire is to live so powerfully right up to the moment of death that I can pass  through that doorway totally alive – with no diminishment.

By contrast, when I fear and avoid death, I don’t really live – every  setback becomes a small death that saps and disables me.

So when I say it means death, I  already mean life. But not life and death.

Spirit

I am bowing to the spirit in the sword – that is my spirit. I am bowing  to myself. Hmm. How does that work?

As I work with the sword, I imbue it with my spirit and it becomes my  sword (we have a strict unwritten rule that no-one can borrow another’s  sword).

Aren’t my body and my sword two different things, though? How can they  be the same?

Well, by the intensity of the practice, I think. I imbue the sword with  my best spirit – and it begins to embody my best, sharpest, most  effective, cleanest self. So my daily little self can bow to my best  self – to recognise that both are part of me, and that they work  together - not in conflict as it so often feels.

And when I perform that bow in a room of onlookers, then I am placing my  best self to be seen, naked to the world. I think that’s what makes it a  powerful ritual – one that feels strange in a room full of people just, y’know, watching, when I’m working with a wooden sword and wearing  jeans.

So that strangeness becomes the lesson: can I be everything written  above in this informal and unusual context. Can I bring everything that  is normally present into this room, rather than being sapped by it?

I don’t know. I can only quieten my smaller self by attending to my  sword. That brings peace. Can I hold that close intensity and see the  whole room? Not easily, as you saw. That’s okay: this is a personal  relationship usually practised privately. In time, as my sword shines  brighter, I’m sure that will become easier.

What I learned was that I can attend to more than the precision of the  movements - I can not only place the sword, but I can touch it too; I  can not only bow forwards, but I can expand backwards as well. I can  extend inwards as much as I reach outwards. I can concentrate closely  and allow people to dance around me. I can also attend to the precision  of my mind, of my relationships, of the room. Maybe, one day, to the precision beyond the room.

When I place the sword, I find myself.
When I touch it, I feel myself.
When I bow to it, I receive myself.
When I reach out, I collect in.
When I concentrate, I notice.
When I am precise, I die.

Oh, what a beautiful life!

A moment in a weekend of embodied exploration – I was working through the first steps of a sword kata in a room full of Alexander Technique students.
