Warm ups aren't warm ups

1 min read
Also in: Aikido

Of course, what we call "warm ups" aren't actually warm ups, they're exercises designed to take slack and stiffness out of the body, and to bring body and mind together. So last night we spent the first hour studying the first few movements in some detail – testing the movements for stability, and attempting to uncover what lines of support the exercises are developing.

Thereafter, standing in aikido posture (hanmi), being shoved out of it by our partner, and recovering the posture in a new place.

Pushes tend to move one's shoulders first, and then the body stumbles to catch up. Stability is lost because we didn't move into the second place calmly. When we direct the push to our one point, then our movement is more centred, and thus better balanced.

Interestingly, it seems that the pusher experiences the push differently when they're pushing on a coordinated body. I'm not sure what to make of that, if anything, but it might be worth noticing.

Finally, a bit of atemi (striking to distract/deflect) as we're pushed backwards. One side of our body yields to the push in order to make space, the other side enters (through intention and awareness, rather than movement). That entering feeling pops the hand up, and the pusher is suddenly off their line.

Quieter sessions like these are among the richest in learning.
