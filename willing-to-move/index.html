Willing to move

2 min read
Also in: Aikido, 1st form, Kotegaeshi

First of all, we did some quiet breathing whilst placing attention at points around the body – One Point (in the abdomen), wrists, fingers, soles of feet, scalp. In all these places, the inhale/exhale cycle was creating quiet expansion and release (contraction is too active a word). Over time we became aware of the whole body expanding and releasing by way of all these locations moving.

Then on to a few Ki development exercises: crossing wrists at One Point, crossing wrists in front of face, swing arms around body, arms out from side (but without bending forward this time), rowing exercise, raising hands to eye level.

In all these exercises, we found a similar expansion and release occurring through the body, this time driven by our intention (extending our mind) rather than our breath.

Why this quiet examination? Because one of the class twisted his back recently, and is still considerably inhibited by it. Part of noticing these subtle movements is tracing them back along their lines to the lower back. We can encourage movement in a stiffened area by moving lightly in other areas - it allows movement within the stiffness, without challenging parts that aren't ready to move.

Last week, we looked at how the nage moves lightly in response to a push or the intention to push. This week, we studied the uke's role a bit more.

We emphasised the holding hand as a listening device, requiring a light, responsive hold rather than a vice-like grip. As the nage walked backwards, the uke was encouraged to move forwards without their grip on the wrist either tightening or loosening, and without their body leaning forwards or backwards - the spine just sitting neutrally atop the pelvis.

As we move, we always have unbendable arm, but it should never be obvious in this exercise - if your partner feels your unbendable arm, it means that you've continued moving after they've stopped. The whole point of this listening-driven movement is to minimise the amount of information going back to the nage.

Finally 1st form kotegaeshi, now with both partners fully performing their role - aiming for total synchronisation of movement, rather than one person leading the other around. Someone commented that they felt nothing when doing the exercise this way, and that's the point. They are giving less information away about their movement/intention, and they are receiving less from their partner.

#willingtomove #movelikeyourlifedependsonit
